<p id="mwCQ"><b id="mwCg">Benzo[<i id="mwCw">c</i>]fluorene</b> is a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon" id="mwDA">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon</a> (PAH) with <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mutagenic" title="Mutagenic" class="mw-redirect" id="mwDQ">mutagenic</a> activity. It is a component of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Coal_tar" title="Coal tar" id="mwDg">coal tar</a>, cigarette smoke and smog and thought to be a major contributor to its <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen" id="mwDw">carcinogenic</a> properties.<sup about="#mwt18" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-major_DNA_1-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;major DNA&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-major_DNA-1&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-major_DNA-1" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 1;" id="mwEA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwEQ">[1]</span></a></sup> The mutagenicity of benzo[<i id="mwEg">c</i>]fluorene is mainly attributed to formation of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./ADME#Metabolism" title="ADME" id="mwEw">metabolites</a> that are reactive and capable of forming <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./DNA_adducts" title="DNA adducts" class="mw-redirect" id="mwFA">DNA adducts</a>.<sup about="#mwt22" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-synthesis_of_suspected_2-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;synthesis of <p id="mwMw">Benzo[<i id="mwNA">c</i>]fluorene occurs naturally in tar, but can also be manually synthesized in a four step process, which is depicted in the picture below. The starting product is <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./1-Indanone" title="1-Indanone" id="mwNQ">1-indanone</a> (1). This is <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Brominated" title="Brominated" class="mw-redirect" id="mwNg">brominated</a> in a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Substitution_reaction" title="Substitution reaction" id="mwNw">substitution reaction</a> to 3-bromoindanone (2) using the reagent <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Bromosuccinimide" title="Bromosuccinimide" class="mw-redirect" id="mwOA"><i id="mwOQ">N</i>-bromosuccinimide</a>. Benzo[<i id="mwPA">c</i>]fluorenone-9 (4) is generated by self-condensation of 2<i id="mwPQ">H</i>-inden-1-one, when heated. The final step is <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Reduction_(chemistry)" title="Reduction (chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" id="mwPg">reduction</a> of this compound with <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hydrazine_hydrate" title="Hydrazine hydrate" class="mw-redirect" id="mwPw">hydrazine hydrate</a>, generating benzo[<i id="mwQA">c</i>]fluorene (5).<sup about="#mwt41" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-concentrations_effect_7-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;concentrations effect&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-concentrations_effect-7&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-concentrations_effect-7" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 7;" id="mwQQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwQg">[7]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="3" id="mwSA"><h2 id="Metabolism">Metabolism</h2>
<p id="mwSQ">In general PAH carcinogenesis involves activation by the enzyme <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./P-450" title="P-450" class="mw-redirect" id="mwSg">P-450</a> to diol epoxide metabolites with an <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Epoxide" title="Epoxide" id="mwSw">epoxide</a> ring in the bay or fjord region. These diol epoxide metabolites are reactive and capable of forming DNA adducts (see the adjacent image).
While benzo[<i id="mwTA">c</i>]fluorene does not have a bay or fjord region it does undergo a similar transformation with a pseudo-bay region that reacts instead. The type of cytochrome P 450 involved is thought to be CYP1A1.<sup about="#mwt46" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-toxicity_of_pah_8-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;toxicity of pah&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-toxicity_of_pah-8&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-toxicity_of_pah-8" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 8;" id="mwTQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwTg">[8]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwTw">The <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Biotransformation" title="Biotransformation" id="mwUA">biotransformation</a> is depicted in the image below. First benzo[<i id="mwUQ">c</i>]fluorene (1) is transformed into trans-3,4-dihydrodiol (2). This substance is transformed by <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cytochrome_P450,_family_1,_member_A1" title="Cytochrome P450, family 1, member A1" class="mw-redirect" id="mwUg">CYP1A1</a> into the highly carcinogenic metabolites <i id="mwUw">anti</i>-diolepoxide (3) and <i id="mwVA">syn</i>-diolepoxide (4).<sup about="#mwt48" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-synthesis_of_suspected_2-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;synthesis of suspected&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-synthesis_of_suspected-2" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 2;" id="mwVQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwVg">[2]</span></a></sup></p>

<section data-mw-section-id="4" id="mwXA"><h3 id="ADME_of_benzo[c]fluorene_and_PAHs_in_general"><span id="ADME_of_benzo.5Bc.5Dfluorene_and_PAHs_in_general" typeof="mw:FallbackId"></span>ADME of benzo[<i id="mwXQ">c</i>]fluorene and PAHs in general</h3>

<section data-mw-section-id="5" id="mwXg"><h4 id="Absorption">Absorption</h4>
<p id="mwXw">Benzo[<i id="mwYA">c</i>]fluorene and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon" id="mwYQ">PAHs</a> in general are mostly absorbed via ingestion, inhalation, and dermal contact. Also, depending on the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Targeted_drug_delivery#Delivery_vehicles" title="Targeted drug delivery" id="mwYg">vehicle</a> (transport medium) in which the PAHs are located, the percentages of absorption can differ. Ingestion of benzo[<i id="mwYw">c</i>]fluorene makes it a very potent lung tumorigen
<sup about="#mwt53" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-potent_systemic_9-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;potent systemic&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-potent_systemic-9&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-potent_systemic-9" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 9;" id="mwZA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwZQ">[9]</span></a></sup>
In particular, benzo[<i id="mwZg">c</i>]fluorene is better absorbed in the lungs.<sup about="#mwt58" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-determination_of_a_10-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;determination of a&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-determination_of_a-10&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-determination_of_a-10" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 10;" id="mwZw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwaA">[10]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="6" id="mwaQ"><h4 id="Distribution">Distribution</h4>
<p id="mwag">Once it is absorbed, benzo[<i id="mwaw">c</i>]fluorene enters the lymph, circulates in the blood and is metabolized. The distribution of PAHs depends on their <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Lipophilicity" title="Lipophilicity" id="mwbA">lipophilicity</a> and probably benzo[<i id="mwbQ">c</i>]fluorene can easily cross the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane" id="mwbg">cell membrane</a>, because of this lipophilicity. This has been proven for similar substances like <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Fluorene" title="Fluorene" id="mwbw">fluorene</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Fluoranthene" title="Fluoranthene" id="mwcA">fluoranthene</a>, but has yet to be investigated for benzo[<i id="mwcQ">c</i>]fluorene.<sup about="#mwt63" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-lipophilic_absorption_11-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;lipophilic absorption&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-lipophilic_absorption-11&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-lipophilic_absorption-11" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 11;" id="mwcg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwcw">[11]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="7" id="mwdA"><h4 id="Metabolism_and_excretion">Metabolism and excretion</h4>
<p id="mwdQ">Benzo[<i id="mwdg">c</i>]fluorene is mainly metabolized by the CYP enzymes in the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Liver" title="Liver" id="mwdw">liver</a>. There is also evidence that a larger number of metabolites are formed in the lungs, which might explain why benzo[<i id="mweA">c</i>]fluorene is such a potent lung tumorigen. It is possible that benzo[<i id="mweQ">c</i>]fluorene may have a unique (and still unknown) mechanism of activation or transportation, which explains why the lungs are targeted.<sup about="#mwt65" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-potent_systemic_9-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;potent systemic&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-potent_systemic-9" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 9;" id="mweg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwew">[9]</span></a></sup>
The initial steps of the metabolism, the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Biotransformation#Phase_.D0.86_reaction" title="Biotransformation" id="mwfA">phase I biotransformation</a>, are described above.</p>

<p id="mwfQ">For many PAHs it has been proven that they are conjugated, in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Biotransformation#Phase_II_reaction" title="Biotransformation" id="mwfg">phase II</a>, with either <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Glucuronide" title="Glucuronide" id="mwfw">glucuronide</a>, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Sulfate" title="Sulfate" id="mwgA">sulfate</a> or <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Glutathione" title="Glutathione" id="mwgQ">glutathione</a>. More research on this topic is necessary for benzo[<i id="mwgg">c</i>]fluorene. Glucuronide and sulfate conjugates of PAH metabolites are generally excreted in the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Bile" title="Bile" id="mwgw">bile</a> and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Urine" title="Urine" id="mwhA">urine</a>.
Glutathione conjugates are further metabolized to mercapturic acids in the kidney and are excreted in the urine. The hydroxylated metabolites of the PAHs are excreted in human urine both as free hydroxylated metabolites and as hydroxylated metabolites conjugated to glucuronic acid and sulfate.<sup about="#mwt67" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-toxicity_of_pah_8-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;toxicity of pah&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-toxicity_of_pah-8" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 8;" id="mwhQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwhg">[8]</span></a></sup></p>

</section></section></section><section data-mw-section-id="8" id="mwhw"><h2 id="Mechanism_of_action">Mechanism of action</h2>
<p id="mwiA">The carcinogenic metabolites of benzo[<i id="mwiQ">c</i>]fluorene bind to DNA which involves the opening of the epoxide ring in benzo[<i id="mwig">c</i>]fluorene anti- and syn-diolepoxide. The benzo[<i id="mwiw">c</i>]fluorene metabolites bind in a yet unknown fashion to the DNA.</p>

<p id="mwjA">When a DNA adduct forms at a site critical to the regulation of cell differentiation or growth it can cause cancer. If an aberration in the DNA is not well repaired by the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Nucleotide_excision_repair" title="Nucleotide excision repair" id="mwjQ">NER</a>, a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mutation" title="Mutation" id="mwjg">mutation</a> will occur during cell replication. In addition, it is known that the cells affected most appear to be those with rapid replication, such as bone marrow, skin, and lung tissue, whereas tissues with slower turnover rate like the liver are less susceptible.<sup about="#mwt70" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-major_DNA_1-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;major DNA&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-major_DNA-1" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 1;" id="mwjw"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwkA">[1]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt71" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-synthesis_of_suspected_2-2" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;synthesis of suspected&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-synthesis_of_suspected-2" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 2;" id="mwkQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwkg">[2]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwkw">Exposure to benzo[<i id="mwlA">c</i>]fluorene in vivo leads to the induction of mainly lung tumors where it acts as a DNA adductor. Lung tumors arise after <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Topical" title="Topical" class="mw-redirect" id="mwlQ">topical</a> application in mice with coal tar, but also when it is ingested. Next to its involvement in lung tumors, benzo[<i id="mwlg">c</i>]fluorene and its metabolites are expected to be involved in the formation of different <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Tumor" title="Tumor" class="mw-redirect" id="mwlw">tumors</a>. The formation of DNA adducts in human breast tumors, hepatoma and colon adenocarcinoma by these metabolites has been shown in vitro. These adducts and the ones that were observed in lung tumors of mice were similar, which strengthens the hypothesis that human cells are capable of forming the mutagenic metabolites.<sup about="#mwt74" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-potent_systemic_9-2" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;potent systemic&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-potent_systemic-9" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 9;" id="mwmA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwmQ">[9]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt78" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-dna_adduct_formation_12-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;dna adduct formation&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-dna_adduct_formation-12&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-dna_adduct_formation-12" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 12;" id="mwmg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwmw">[12]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="9" id="mwnA"><h2 id="Environmental_exposure">Environmental exposure</h2>
<p id="mwnQ">Benzo[<i id="mwng">c</i>]fluorene belongs to a group of compounds called polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). PAHs and their derivatives are ubiquitous in the environment and they are produced in several industrial and <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Combustion" title="Combustion" id="mwnw">combustion</a> processes.<sup about="#mwt83" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-urban_air_13-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;urban air&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-urban_air-13&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-urban_air-13" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 13;" id="mwoA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwoQ">[13]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwog">Workers in industries or trades using or producing <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Coal_combustion_products" title="Coal combustion products" id="mwow">coal</a>, crude oil or coal products are at highest risk for PAH exposure. In general, the PAHs are formed during these industrial processes by incomplete combustion or pyrolysis of organic matter. The higher the temperature the more PAHs are formed.<sup about="#mwt89" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-gezondheidsraad_14-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;gezondheidsraad&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-gezondheidsraad-14&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-gezondheidsraad-14" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 14;" id="mwpA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwpQ">[14]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwpg">Some of these PAHs, such as benzo[<i id="mwpw">c</i>]fluorene, are carcinogens and mutagens and act as possible <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology" id="mwqA">endocrine</a> disruptors. To estimate the health effects that arise from exposure to PAHs and benzo[<i id="mwqQ">c</i>]fluorene it is necessary to determine the concentration of these compounds in the atmosphere. This was done in a study by Morisaki et al. 2016. They compared the concentrations of different PAHs including benzo[<i id="mwqg">c</i>]fluorene in Beijing and Kanazawa in winter and summer.</p>

<p id="mwATI">The researchers corrected for the relative mutagenicity of compounds compared to <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Benzo(a)pyrene" title="Benzo(a)pyrene" id="mwATM">benzo[<i id="mwATQ">a</i>]pyrene</a>.<sup about="#mwt153" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-development_of_15-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;development of&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-development_of-15&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-development_of-15" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 15;" id="mwATU"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwATY">[15]</span></a></sup> The results of this are presented as BaPeq, which equals the concentration of the compound, times the potency of the compound compared to benzo[<i id="mwATc">a</i>]pyrene (RPF). </section><section data-mw-section-id="10" id="mwATw"><h2 id="Safety">Safety</h2>

<section data-mw-section-id="11" id="mwAT0"><h3 id="Carcinogenity">Carcinogenity</h3>
<p id="mwAT4">In one study, the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Ames_test" title="Ames test" id="mwAT8">Ames test</a> was performed on benzo[<i id="mwAUA">c</i>]fluorene. Two different strains were used, TA100 and TA98. One group of each strain had a rat liver fraction and one group did not. The difference between the TA100 and the TA98 strain is that the TA98 strain has a frameshift mutation, and the TA100 has a base substitution mutation. When the amount of benzo[<i id="mwAUE">c</i>]fluorene is increased in TA 100 yeast strain, the amount of revertants per plate does not increase. Only in the TA98 strain plate, which contained a fraction of a rat liver, a higher dose of benzo[<i id="mwAUI">c</i>]fluorene seems to correspond with a larger amount of revertants. This indicates that benzo[<i id="mwAUM">c</i>]fluorene is metabolized by enzymes in the rat liver into more potent mutagenic compounds. These compounds only affected the TA98 strain. This indicates that the adducts formed by benzo[<i id="mwAUQ">c</i>]fluorene metabolites cause <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Frameshift_mutation" title="Frameshift mutation" id="mwAUU">frameshift mutations</a>, and not <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Point_mutation" title="Point mutation" id="mwAUY">point mutations</a>.<sup about="#mwt159" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-mutagenicity_16-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;mutagenicity&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-mutagenicity-16&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-mutagenicity-16" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 16;" id="mwAUc"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAUg">[16]</span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="12" id="mwAV4"><h3 id="Effects_on_animals">Effects on animals</h3>
<p id="mwAV8">In one animal study, mice that were fed <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Coal_tar" title="Coal tar" id="mwAWA">coal tar</a> developed lung tumors. DNA adducts in these mice were analyzed and could be traced back to benzo[<i id="mwAWE">c</i>]fluorene. This and another similar study suggest a contribution of benzo[<i id="mwAWI">c</i>]fluorene to the carcinogenic potency of coal tar when administered orally.<sup about="#mwt167" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-major_DNA_1-2" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;major DNA&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-major_DNA-1" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 1;" id="mwAWM"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAWQ">[1]</span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt171" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-comparative_analysis_18-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;comparative analysis&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-comparative_analysis-18&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-comparative_analysis-18" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 18;" id="mwAWU"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAWY">[18]</span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwAWc">Another study found that benzo[<i id="mwAWg">c</i>]fluorene is also carcinogenic in mice when applied <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Topically" title="Topically" class="mw-redirect" id="mwAWk">topically</a>, inducing lung and skin cancer. Of the results of this study a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Dose–response_relationship" title="Dose–response relationship" id="mwAWo">dose-response curve</a> has been made, see the image above.</p>
 
<p id="mwAWs">This figure shows the DNA adduct level after a certain dose of benzo[<i id="mwAWw">c</i>]fluorene was applied to the skin of mice. This level is similar in the lungs and in the skin implying that benzo[<i id="mwAW0">c</i>]fluorene is a systemic <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Mutagen" title="Mutagen" id="mwAW4">mutagen</a>.<sup about="#mwt176" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-comparatice_in_vitro_17-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;comparatice in vitro&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-comparatice_in_vitro-17&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-comparatice_in_vitro-17" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 17;" id="mwAW8"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAXA">[17]</span></a></sup></p>
 
<p id="mwAXE">The effects of exposure to benzo[<i id="mwAXI">c</i>]fluorene were also researched on rats. In one of these studies the liver was established to be the main place of disposition of benzo[<i id="mwAXM">c</i>]fluorene after a single oral dose regardless of the size of the dose. It was found that 55-69% of the labelled benzo[<i id="mwAXQ">c</i>]fluorene was excreted via the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Feces" title="Feces" id="mwAXU">feces</a> while 8–10% was found to be eliminated via urine. While the benzo[<i id="mwAXY">c</i>]fluorene found in the feces was not biotransformed, the urine samples mainly showed polar metabolites of benzo[<i id="mwAXc">c</i>]fluorene.<sup about="#mwt181" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-disposition_19-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw="{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;ref&quot;,&quot;attrs&quot;:{&quot;name&quot;:&quot;disposition&quot;},&quot;body&quot;:{&quot;id&quot;:&quot;mw-reference-text-cite_note-disposition-19&quot;}}"><a href="./Benzo(c)fluorene#cite_note-disposition-19" style="counter-reset: mw-Ref 19;" id="mwAXg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwAXk">[19]</span></a></sup></p>
